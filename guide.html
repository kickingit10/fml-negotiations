What Is All This? Start Here ▼

Final Mile Logistics, Inc (FML) is a FedEx Ground ISP (Independent Service Provider). FML gets paid by FedEx to deliver packages under a contract that defines the rates per stop, per package, per week, etc.

Contracts don't last forever — they expire and get renegotiated. These tools exist to help you make the best decision on your next contract by comparing the options FedEx has offered using your actual operating data, not just FedEx's estimates.

Two separate negotiations are covered here:
1. OLD negotiation — Neg Req 1000045949, which resulted in your current contract C8882360. This is already signed. The Performance page shows how that negotiation worked out.
2. NEW negotiation — Neg Req 1000055001, for new contract C8892366 (Jun 2026 – Aug 2028). This is the one you need to decide on. Deadline: March 24, 2026.

FedEx doesn't offer a single "take it or leave it" contract. Instead, they give you 3 different options called MESOs (Multiple Equivalent Simultaneous Offers). Each MESO has different rate structures — some pay more per stop but less in fixed weekly charges, and vice versa. Your job is to figure out which one pays you the most given your actual workload.

Understanding the Analyzer Sections Deep Dive ▼

The C8892366 Analyzer has 6 sections. Here's what each one does and why it matters:

Section 1: Volume Transparency

Compares FedEx's estimated volume (what they think you'll deliver) vs your actual trailing volume (what you've really been doing for 49 weeks). This matters because MESO revenue depends heavily on volume — if FedEx underestimates your stops, the MESO that looks best on paper might not be best in reality.

Section 2: Revenue Calculator

Shows the total revenue each MESO would generate and how it compares to your current contract. You can toggle between three volume scenarios: FedEx's projection, your 49-week average, and your recent 12-week trend. This catches seasonal shifts — maybe your recent volume is higher or lower than your long-term average.

Section 3: Rate Impact Sliders

Your counter-offer workshop. Pick a MESO as your starting point, then drag any rate up or down. The right side instantly shows the total revenue and how it compares to all 3 MESOs. Use this to answer questions like: "If I can get them to raise the stop charge by $0.10, is that worth more than a $500/wk higher service charge?"

Section 4: Break-Even Analysis

The safety check. For each MESO, it calculates how far your volume would need to drop before that MESO earns less than your current contract. A MESO with 20% break-even means even if you lose 20% of your volume, you're still earning more than today. Higher-fixed-cost MESOs (like MESO 1) tend to have better break-even because the fixed service charge protects you when volume dips.

Section 5: Contract Period Impact

Goes rate-by-rate and shows the total dollar difference vs your current contract. A $0.04 stop rate increase doesn't sound like much — but multiplied by your weekly stops for 52 weeks, that adds up fast. This section does that math for every rate component so you can see which ones actually move the needle.

Section 6: Full Rate Table

The raw numbers. Every rate for every MESO across all 3 years (Year 1, Year 2, Year 3). Year 2/3 rates sometimes escalate — this table highlights those changes in amber so you don't miss them. Always check Year 2+ rates before making a decision based only on Year 1. Note: for FML's old negotiation, Old MESO 2 had identical rates to Final Approval — effectively making Old MESO 1 the only meaningfully different option.

52-Week vs 110-Week View Important ▼

The analyzer has a toggle at the top that switches between two views:

52-Week Annualized View Full 110-Week Contract

52-Week Annualized

Shows what you'd earn in one year using Year 1 rates only. Easier to compare against your current annual revenue. This is the default view and the one most people should use for decision-making.

Full 110-Week Contract

Shows the total value over the entire contract — Y1=52 wks + Y2=52 wks + Y3=6 wks = 110 total. Includes rate escalation in Years 2-3. Use this when you want to see the full financial picture including how rates change over time.

When does the view matter? For most decisions, the 52-week view is all you need. Switch to 110-week when you want to factor in Year 2/3 rate escalation — for example, MESO 1's service charge jumps from $8,850/wk in Year 1 to $9,536/wk in Year 2. That $686/wk increase over 58 weeks is an extra ~$39,788 that only shows up in the 110-week view.

Key Concepts Terminology ▼

What is a MESO?

Multiple Equivalent Simultaneous Offers. FedEx presents 3 contract options at once. They're designed to be roughly equivalent in total value but structured differently — one might pay more per stop but less in fixed charges, another might be the opposite. Your actual workload determines which one is actually best for you.

Fixed vs Variable Revenue

Fixed charges (service charge, apparel, vehicle brand) are the same every week regardless of volume. Variable charges (stop, package, fuel, e-com) go up or down based on how many packages you deliver. A high-fixed MESO protects you if volume drops. A high-variable MESO rewards you if volume grows. Your current volume trend tells you which matters more.

FedEx Volume vs Your Actual Volume

When FedEx builds the MESO offers, they use their own volume projections. But their estimates might be wrong — maybe they're projecting fewer e-commerce stops than you actually handle. Since variable revenue depends on volume, using the wrong volume gives you the wrong answer about which MESO is best. That's why every analysis here lets you switch between FedEx's numbers and your real data.

Rate Escalation (Year 2/3)

MESO rates aren't flat for the whole contract. Some components increase in Year 2 and Year 3. For example, service charges often go up while per-stop rates stay flat. Always check the Full Rate Table to see which rates change — a MESO that looks great in Year 1 might be even better (or slightly worse) when escalation kicks in.

Advance CSC (Cash Sign-on)

Some MESOs include a one-time advance payment (like $14,000) paid when you sign. This is "free money" upfront but it's built into the deal — you're not getting it for nothing. The analyzer includes it in total revenue calculations so you can see the full picture.

Break-Even Point

The percentage your volume can drop before a new MESO starts paying less than your current contract. Think of it as your safety margin. If a MESO has a 25% break-even, you'd need to lose a quarter of your business before it becomes a bad deal. Higher = safer.

Rate Glossary Quick Reference ▼

Every rate component in your FedEx contract, explained in plain English:

Service Charge — Fixed weekly payment. Your "base salary" from FedEx regardless of volume.
Stop Charge — Paid per non-e-commerce delivery stop. More residential/business stops = more revenue.
Fuel Surcharge — Per-stop supplement for fuel costs. Applied to ALL stops (non-e-com + e-com).
Package Charge — Paid per non-e-commerce package. Multi-package stops earn more than single-package stops.
LP Mix Charge — "Large Package" supplement. Paid per package that qualifies as oversized/heavy. Higher LP % = more revenue.
E-Com Stop Charge — Per-stop payment for e-commerce (SmartPost/SurePost) deliveries. Usually lower rate than regular stops.
E-Com Package Charge — Per-package payment for e-commerce deliveries. Separate from the stop charge.
Apparel Charge — Fixed weekly payment for driver uniform/appearance compliance.
Vehicle Brand Charge — Per-vehicle weekly payment for maintaining FedEx-branded vehicles.
Surge Stop — Higher per-stop rate that kicks in during peak volume periods (holidays, etc.).
Early Pull — Per-event payment when FedEx requests early departure from the terminal.
Liquidated Damages — Per-package penalty FedEx can charge for service failures. Lower = better for you.
Advance CSC — One-time upfront cash payment included in some MESOs. Only paid in Year 1.
Vehicles — Number of branded vehicles in the contract. Affects vehicle brand charge total (rate x count).

Negotiation Tips Strategy ▼

Compare apples to apples. Always use the same volume assumption when comparing MESOs. Toggle all cards to "My Actual Volume" — FedEx's projection is designed to make their offers look balanced, not to show you which one is actually best for your operation.
Don't just look at total revenue. Two MESOs might produce similar totals but one might be 70% variable (risky if volume drops) while the other is 50% fixed (more stable). Check the break-even analysis to understand your downside.
Use the sliders to build your counter. Start from the best MESO and push specific rates higher. "I like MESO 3's structure but need the stop rate to $2.85 instead of $2.75" — the slider shows you the exact dollar impact instantly. That's your negotiation ammunition.
Check the Year 2/3 escalation. The Full Rate Table (Section 6 of the Analyzer) shows exactly which rates change. A MESO that's #2 in Year 1 might become #1 when Year 2 rates kick in. Use the 110-week view to capture this.
The advance is a one-time sweetener. $14,000 sounds great, but spread over 110 weeks it's only $127/week. Don't let it distract from the rates you'll live with for 2+ years. Check total revenue both with and without the advance.
Remember: You've done this before. The C8882360 Performance page shows what your last negotiation was worth. Use that confidence going in — the data says negotiating works.
